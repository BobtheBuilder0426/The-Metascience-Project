# Reasoning trace — how I got from question to edaravone

## The decision rule (fixed before looking at chemistry)
> **Nominate the compound that pharmacologically hits the single node on which the most *independent* public-data lines converge, subject to: (a) a resolvable identifier, (b) plausible brain exposure, (c) no prior report in primary complex-I disease (novelty), (d) an acceptable safety profile.**

This rule is deliberately mechanism-first and anti-cherry-pick: the node is chosen by data convergence, and only then is a molecule selected to fit it. It also builds in a novelty test and a safety gate so I don't nominate a repurposing candidate that is either already claimed or obviously toxic.

## Step 1 — Lock the target and the landscape
- **Disease:** MONDO:0100133 (mitochondrial complex I deficiency), confirmed via ontology search; nuclear type-1 subtype MONDO:0100224.
- **Targets:** Open Targets returns 2420 associated genes; the top scorers are structural subunits (NDUFS1 0.835, NDUFS4 0.832, NDUFS2, NDUFV1) and assembly factors (FOXRED1, NUBPL). Crucially the disease has **0 approved/candidate drugs** in Open Targets — quantifying the whitespace.
- **Lesion classes:** ClinVar/gnomAD confirms all three genetic routes (nuclear structural, assembly factors, mtDNA ND). This matters because a good candidate should act **downstream of the lesion**, on shared pathophysiology, so it can help regardless of which gene is mutated. (P/LP counts were tallied strictly — Pathogenic + Likely-pathogenic + Pathogenic/Likely-pathogenic only, excluding "Conflicting classifications": NDUFV1 68, NDUFS1 50, NDUFS4 47, ACAD9 184, NDUFAF5 118, FOXRED1 62, NUBPL 20.)
- **Model choice:** GTEx shows NDUFS4 is highly expressed in cultured fibroblasts (115.8 TPM) — validating the patient-fibroblast model I later lean on.

## Step 2 — Choose the mechanistic axis
I classified every landscape agent as trying to (i) raise the **NAD pool**, (ii) **shuttle electrons/ROS**, or (iii) reprogram metabolism. The gap I pushed: the rigorous mechanistic literature (Titov 2016 LbNOX; Sullivan 2015 aspartate/electron-acceptor) says the failing variable is **NAD⁺ regeneration kinetics / electron-acceptor supply**, i.e. **reductive stress** — not NAD pool size. So I looked for the *druggable consequence* of reductive stress, rather than trying (again) to top up NAD.

## Step 3 — Let the data pick the node (not my memory)
Three independent public lines were required to agree before I would commit to a node:

1. **My own cross-lab signature.** I did not trust any single dataset. I built differential expression from two independent labs/regions (GSE149616 cerebellum; GSE242286 cortex/hippocampus), then kept only the **direction-concordant** genes (466 of 605 shared-significant; ρ=0.29 genome-wide). This is a batch-robustness go/no-go — it passed. The correctly-signed, unambiguous signal is **down-regulation of NADPH-dependent aldehyde reductases (AKR1B1/B10/E1)** — a loss of capacity to clear the reactive aldehydes generated by lipid peroxidation.
   - **A direction caveat I had to get right:** the signature also shows *ACSL4/ACSL6* down. ACSL4 is **pro-ferroptotic** (it esterifies polyunsaturated fatty acids into membrane phospholipids, the substrate for peroxidation). So down-regulating ACSL4 *reduces* peroxidisable substrate — it is a **compensatory/adaptive** move, not a marker of cells "losing." I therefore do **not** use ACSL4/6-down as evidence of vulnerability; the vulnerability rests on the detox-capacity loss (AKR) plus the two orthogonal lines below.
2. **An unbiased genetic dependency.** To 2019's genome-wide CRISPR compendium independently flags **GPX4** (anti-ferroptosis) as synthetic sick/lethal with mito inhibition. This is orthogonal evidence: not expression, but a survival dependency.
3. **A human patient-cell response.** GSE27041 fibroblasts show Nrf2/glutathione/selenoprotein induction — the antioxidant arm of the same axis, in humans.

All three name **lipid peroxidation / ferroptosis from reductive stress**. Convergence across data *types* (expression, dependency, patient cells) is much stronger than any one screen, and it is the reason I am comfortable committing.

## Step 4 — The signature-reversal question, and the honest NOT-RUN
I wanted an L1000/CMap connectivity reversal: find compounds whose transcriptional signature *opposes* the disease signature. I could not run it: CLUE.io is proxy-blocked and the raw L1000 matrices (~40 GB) do not fit in the available memory, and I refuse to fabricate connectivity scores. **I recorded this as NOT-RUN.** In its place, GSE149616 offered something arguably better: a **genetic** signature-reversal arm (NDI1 reoxidises NADH). Its honest result — rescues lifespan but barely moves the brain transcriptome (4 genes) — both supports the reductive-stress mechanism *and* warns that upstream NADH-reoxidation alone is not sufficient in brain, motivating a downstream (lipid-peroxidation) intervention.

## Step 5 — Fit a molecule to the node, then stress-test it
Given node = lipid peroxidation/ferroptosis, the candidate classes are: radical-trapping antioxidants (edaravone, tocopherol), lipoxygenase/ferroptosis inhibitors (vatiquinone, ferrostatin/liproxstatin tool compounds), iron chelators (deferiprone), quinones (idebenone), redox cyclers (methylene blue). I pulled physicochemistry + safety + approval status for all of them.
- **Edaravone won** on the conjunction: mechanism fit (documented anti-ferroptosis/lipid-peroxidation in a neuronal model, PMID 36333599; activity against the complex-I poison MPP⁺ in astrocytes via the apoptotic pathway, PMID 18643790), drug-like size with low lipophilicity, **approved CNS drug (BBB proven empirically)**, clean safety, and — the novelty gate — **zero literature in primary CI disease**.
- **Rejected / demoted:** tool inhibitors (ferrostatin-1, liproxstatin-1) are not drugs and lack CNS data; alpha-tocopherol/idebenone/vatiquinone are far more lipophilic; deferiprone carries a black-box agranulocytosis warning → backup only; methylene blue is already well-trodden in mito disease.

## Step 6 — The counter-evidence that shaped the experiment
Examining the vatiquinone citations produced the most important finding in the whole analysis. Vatiquinone (EPI-743) targets the same GPX4/15-lipoxygenase ferroptosis axis and, in patient cells, potently prevents RSL3-induced ferroptosis (PMID 30921410). But a preclinical evaluation (PMID 38883711, preprint) found vatiquinone **rescued the RSL3 cell assay yet did not improve survival in the Ndufs4 Leigh mouse in vivo** (possibly reducing seizures). A ferroptosis-node antioxidant that passes the exact cell assay I had proposed has **already failed in vivo in the closest model**. This does not kill the hypothesis — edaravone differs in mechanism (broad radical trapping vs a lipoxygenase-axis quinone), lipophilicity and CNS-exposure profile — but it forces the experiment to include a mandatory in-vivo tier on the endpoints vatiquinone failed, and it is stated openly as the strongest argument against the nomination.

## Step 7 — Guard against my own hallucination
Every named paper, screen, accession and identifier in the answer was **queried directly against a connector**, not carried from memory:
- Each PMID was fetched and its abstract read to confirm it supports the exact sentence it is attached to (e.g. checking the single edaravone-"Leigh" hit is actually MELAS management, not primary CI; confirming PMID 18643790 is astrocytes via apoptosis, not neuronal ferroptosis; confirming the vatiquinone in-vivo failure).
- Every compound has a PubChem CID + InChIKey + (where applicable) ChEMBL ID, verified live.
- Every GEO accession was opened, its design and supplementary count file confirmed, and the counts actually downloaded and analysed.
- ClinVar P/LP counts were tallied directly from the variant records with an explicit rule (excluding "Conflicting classifications").
- The one line I could not run is labelled NOT-RUN, not filled with invented numbers.

## What would make me wrong (and how the first experiment tests it)
If CI-deficient cells are **not** hypersensitive to GPX4 inhibition, or edaravone does **not** shift the RSL3 EC₅₀ preferentially in CI-deficient cells, the mechanistic chain is broken. But because vatiquinone already cleared the cell tier and failed in vivo, the real test is the **in-vivo tier**: edaravone must improve survival/neuropathology in the Ndufs4 Leigh mouse — the endpoints vatiquinone missed. The design includes an NDI1/LbNOX reversal control so a positive result is mechanistically interpretable rather than a nonspecific antioxidant effect, and explicit kill criteria at both tiers.
