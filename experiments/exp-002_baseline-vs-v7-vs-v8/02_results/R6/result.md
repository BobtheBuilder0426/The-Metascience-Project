# Why does biological aging occur?
## A unifying hypothesis: aging is open-loop drift of the body's regulatory set-points

---

> **Bottom line (≤150 words).** Natural selection can only tune a control system against targets it can "see." Hamilton's result — the force of selection falls to zero after reproduction (PMID 6015424) — means there is **no selected set-point for the post-reproductive body**. So the homeostatic loops that hold youthful physiology in place are calibrated only through early adulthood; afterwards they run **open-loop**, continuing the multiplicative *developmental* trajectory instead of defending a fixed target (quasi-program, PMID 17012837). Aging is the accumulating **control error** of that drift. This one premise generates the Gompertz curve and its late-life plateau, the naked mole-rat exception, the shared hallmarks, why *lowering* growth/nutrient signaling extends life while *antioxidants* do not (NIA ITP: 0/8 antioxidants work; held-out accuracy 94%, p=4×10⁻⁴), and why reprogramming resets age. It predicts youthful function is **recoverable by restoring the regulatory input alone — damage and the epigenetic clock left untouched**.

## The question, stated precisely

"Why does aging occur?" has two halves that a deep answer must fuse: the **ultimate** (evolutionary) question — why hasn't selection eliminated a trait that kills its carrier? — and the **proximate** (mechanistic) question — what physical process produces the progressive, age-synchronized decline we measure? Most theories answer one half well and bolt the other on. I looked for a single premise that *derives* the second from the first, then tested it against a seven-part constraint set (C1 evolutionary logic; C2 mortality kinetics; C3 cross-species variation; C4 conserved hallmarks; C5 what interventions work; C6 reset/rejuvenation; C7 program-vs-damage placement).

## The hypothesis

**Aging is the open-loop drift of the organism's homeostatic set-points.** Homeostatic controllers (nutrient sensing, endocrine axes, immune tone, epigenetic maintenance) are tuned by selection to hold a youthful operating point — but only over the window where selection acts. Because the force of selection decays to zero across reproductive life (PMID 6015424; Medawar 1952; antagonistic pleiotropy, Williams 1957, DOI 10.1111/j.1558-5646.1957.tb02911.x), **there is no target state the post-reproductive body is selected to return to.** The loops do not switch off — they lose their reference and keep extrapolating the multiplicative developmental trajectory. Aging is the growing gap between where the controllers *drive* the body and where a youthful set-point *would* hold it; damage accumulates as a *consequence* of running open-loop, not as the primary driver.

This is a control-theoretic reframing of Blagosklonny's hyperfunction/quasi-program idea (PMID 17012837): hyperfunction is *what* open-loop control looks like at the pathway level; "no selected reference for the aged state" is *why* it happens. The novelty is not vocabulary — it is a specific, falsifiable claim about the **reversibility structure** of aging (below).

## How one premise meets all seven constraints

**C1 — Evolutionary logic (predicts).** Hamilton's result strictly licenses one step: the force of selection falls to zero after reproduction (PMID 6015424), so alleles maintaining a youthful operating point are *unselected* in the aged body. Turning "unselected reference" into "drifting reference that continues the developmental direction" requires three explicit physiological **posits**, not theorems: (P1) the operating point is a *maintained* quantity that decays without selection; (P2) it drifts *along the developmental trajectory*, not randomly (the quasi-program); (P3) damage is downstream of the resulting control error. These posits — formalized as a control model in `causal_model_spec.txt` — are the hypothesis's falsifiable content; the evolutionary step alone does not entail them. Given them, aging is the default of an unrefereed controller.

**C2 — Mortality kinetics (explains; sufficiency, not derivation).** If dysregulation grows multiplicatively, per-individual hazard is h(t)=a·e^{bt} — a **Gompertz law** — and between-individual variation in loop integrity (Gamma-distributed b) yields the observed **late-life plateau** by frailty selection (high-drift individuals die first, decelerating survivors' hazard: Carey 1992, PMID 1411540; Curtsinger 1992, PMID 1411541; Barbi 2018, PMID 29954979 — contested, PMID 30262471 / 30337380). Fig 2 generates both from one parameter. **Scored *explains*:** the simulation *assumes* an exponential individual hazard, so it shows drift is *sufficient* for these kinetics but does not derive the Gompertz law, and the plateau is a generic frailty effect.

**C3 — Cross-species variation (explains).** The same knob *b* — how long selection keeps the loop closed — sets the trajectory: species with strong post-maturity selection (naked mole-rats: eusocial, protected, late reproduction) would keep the loop closed and show a **flat, non-Gompertzian hazard** (Ruby 2018, PMID 29364116; upheld with doubled data 2024, PMID 38773057), and the >1000-fold lifespan range (Jones 2014, PMID 24317695) becomes variation in one thing: how long the reference persists. **Scored *explains*:** this is a consistent account, but the NMR flatness is equally consistent with several theories and I have not derived it independently.

**C4 — Conserved hallmarks (explains co-occurrence).** The hallmarks (López-Otín 2013, PMID 23746838; 2023, PMID 36599349) are puzzling *because they arrive together*. Under drift they must: they are correlated **readouts of one drifting control state**, not nine independent clocks. This is why physiological dysregulation is a single conserved signature across primates (Cohen 2019, PMID 30746836). (Scored *explains*, not *predicts*: the co-occurrence account is post-hoc — I have not derived the hallmark set from the model.)

**C5 — What works (predicts; the decisive, non-circular test).** If aging is control error, interventions that **re-impose a youthful reference** — lowering growth/nutrient/inflammatory signaling — should work, while interventions that only **mop up byproducts** (antioxidants) should not. I tested this on the NIA Interventions Testing Program: a *frozen, outcome-defined* set with protocol-matched negatives. Assigning each compound's mechanism direction **from ChEMBL pharmacology before looking at outcomes**, the direction predicts lifespan extension with **94% leave-one-out held-out accuracy (permutation p=4×10⁻⁴)**: signaling-lowering/hormetic 8/9 positive; **antioxidant/damage-buffer 0/8** (positives: rapamycin PMID 19587680, acarbose/17α-estradiol/NDGA PMID 24245565, aspirin PMID 18631321, glycine PMID 30916479, canagliflozin PMID 32990681; antioxidant nulls PMID 22451473 / 24245565) (Fig 1). This is exactly the pattern the **free-radical theory gets backwards** (Harman 1956, PMID 13332224). Honest exceptions, kept in view: **metformin alone (0.1%) did not extend lifespan** though metformin+rapamycin did (Strong 2016, PMID 27312235) — a signaling drug that failed, counting *against* my rule; and **aspirin** is a mixed case (male-only extension in the 2008 cohort, PMID 18631321, but no benefit at two doses in the 2019 cohort, PMID 30916479). The result is robust to this: dropping aspirin gives 7/8 vs 0/8 (Fisher p=7×10⁻⁴, held-out 94%), and even coding aspirin as a *failure* gives 7/9 vs 0/8 (Fisher p=1.9×10⁻³, held-out 88%) — both still significant at p<0.01. A complementary check confirms the convergence is **functional, not structural**: the positive-intervention protein targets do **not** form a tighter STRING network than the negatives' (max degree 8/13, density 0.35 vs 0.67), so PPI topology does not separate winners from failures — only mechanism *direction* does. Because the mechanism coding is the load-bearing step, I had it re-done **blind to outcome** by an independent classifier working only from ChEMBL pharmacology: it reproduced my labels 16/17 (94%), and re-running on those independent labels still separates the classes (8/10 vs 0/7, Fisher p=1.9×10⁻³, held-out 88%). The one disagreement (resveratrol coded hormetic rather than antioxidant) *weakens* the rule if anything, yet it survives.

**C6 — Reset/rejuvenation (explains).** If age is a drifted control state rather than irreversible damage, **rewriting the state should restore youth without first repairing damage** — and partial reprogramming does exactly that (vision after optic-nerve injury: Lu 2020, PMID 33268865; progeroid lifespan and tissue recovery: Ocampo 2016, PMID 27984723; transient human-fibroblast reprogramming: Gill 2022, PMID 35390271), and the epigenetic clock resets to near-zero in the early embryo (Kerepesi 2021, PMID 34172448; Horvath 2013, PMID 24138928). Damage theories cannot easily explain reset; drift accommodates it. (Scored *explains*: this evidence is shared with the epigenetic-noise theory and does not uniquely discriminate.)

**C7 — Program vs damage (dissolves the dichotomy).** Drift is *quasi-programmed by derivation*: deterministic in its direction (hence clock-like regularity and resettability), yet not an adaptive program selected to kill. It sits precisely where the evidence points — between Gladyshev's imperfectness-driven damage (DOI 10.1016/j.tig.2013.05.004) and Gems & Partridge's programmatic reading (PMID 23190075).

## The novel, discriminating prediction

The theories split on **reversibility structure**, and stating the discriminator precisely is what makes the prediction non-trivial. *Irreversible-lesion* theories — stochastic damage (Harman 1956), somatic mutation (Cagan 2022, PMID 35418684), disposable soma (Kirkwood 1977, PMID 593350) — predict function cannot be restored without removing the lesion. But two theories **do** predict recoverability, and I must not claim to beat them by fiat:

- **Epigenetic-information loss** (Yang 2023, PMID 36638792; erratum DOI 10.1016/j.cell.2024.01.049) explicitly predicts restoration — but via OSK reprogramming that **rewrites the epigenome and resets the clock** (Lu 2020, PMID 33268865, asks precisely "whether older individuals retain the information needed to restore these patterns"). Function returns *with* the clock reset.
- **Hyperfunction** (Blagosklonny 2006, PMID 17012837) predicts recovery by lowering growth signaling — open-loop drift **shares** this; it is a sibling, not discriminated.

So the discriminating claim is narrower than "aging is reversible": **restoring the regulatory *input* rescues function at a *fixed epigenetic clock and fixed damage load*.** That splits open-loop drift from information-loss (needs the clock to move) and lesion theories (need damage removed), though not from hyperfunction. Closest prior art — loss of *resilience* with age (Pyrkov 2021, PMID 34035236; Cohen 2018, PMID 29939206; critical slowing, Olde Rikkert 2016, PMID 26765499), "mallostasis" mean-reverting set-point models (PMID 38092834), and Dilman's neuroendocrine "elevation" model (PMID 381162) — establishes the drift *correlation*; my addition is the **causal, order-of-operations claim** that resilience is recoverable by input restoration *before and independent of* both clock reset and damage clearance.

## Falsifier and the smallest pilot

**Falsified if:** in an aged cell/tissue system, acutely restoring a youthful regulatory input fails to rescue a functional readout unless damage is first removed *or the epigenetic clock is first reset* — i.e. rescue tracks damage clearance or clock movement, not the input itself.

**Pilot (single researcher, ~8 weeks, ordinary budget; see reasoning.md for detail):** In replicatively-aged primary human fibroblasts, use an **inducible, reversible** lowering of mTOR/growth signaling (rapamycin pulse) and measure a functional set-point readout (autophagic flux, proteostasis reporter, nucleocytoplasmic transport) **at fixed damage load** (γH2AX, lipofuscin) **and fixed epigenetic age** (a rapid methylation-clock or transcriptomic-clock readout on the same wells). *Necessity+rescue design:* if function recovers **within hours–days while both damage markers and the clock are unchanged**, drift (recoverable control error) is supported *and* separated from information-loss (which requires the clock to move); if function only recovers as damage falls or the clock resets, drift is refuted in favour of the lesion/information camp. A parallel arm withdrawing the input tests re-drift. The fixed-clock readout is the specific addition that makes this discriminate open-loop drift from epigenetic-information loss — not just from damage theories — at the level of *timing and order*, which no existing ITP or clock study resolves.

## Honest limits

The C5 test rests on N=17 (small), with aspirin a non-replicated case (result robust to it, above). Under a scoring deliberately guarded against inflating my own candidate, the hypothesis **predicts** two constraints — C1 (evolutionary derivation, modulo the three posits above) and C5 (the held-out ITP test, p=4×10⁻⁴) — and **explains** five (C2/C3 shown only as *sufficiency*, not a derivation; C4, C6, C7, several shared with rivals), for a fit of **8**, ahead of the best rival (hyperfunction, 4) with zero contradictions. The kinetics are not fits to primary life tables (SSA data was not machine-accessible; access was granted but the server refused the default client, and I did not spoof a browser). The discriminating prediction is decisive **only against the lesion and information-loss camps, not hyperfunction** (a sibling), rests on the fixed-clock/fixed-damage condition, and has not yet been run. The edge is **unification and the evolutionary derivation** — not a new molecule, and not a tighter molecular network.
