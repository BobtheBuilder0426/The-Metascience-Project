# The Cofactor Ratchet

### A hypothesis for how a soup of lifeless organic molecules became the first self-replicating cell

---

## The one-sentence answer

**Compartment, metabolism, and heredity were never three separate inventions that had to find each other — they were three faces of a single object, a chemically-active droplet, and the pivot that turned that droplet into a cell was a class of molecules that is still sitting in every living thing today: the nucleotide cofactors (the ancestors of ATP, NAD, FAD, coenzyme A, SAM). The same molecules that ran the droplet's metabolism were nucleotides, so when they were crowded together on the droplet's scaffolds they occasionally joined end-to-end into the first templates. Genetic heredity did not have to be conjured from nothing; it was handed off from a cruder heredity that already existed — the *composition* of the droplet — at exactly the moment that cruder heredity ran out of room.**

We call the mechanism the **Cofactor Ratchet**, because every step is a small, chemically-favoured increment that cannot easily slip backwards, and because the cofactors are the teeth that let the system climb from lifeless chemistry to open-ended evolution without a single improbable leap.

## Why the origin of life looks impossible (and why that is an illusion of framing)

The problem is usually stated as a chicken-and-egg knot with three eggs:

1. **Heredity needs metabolism.** A gene (RNA) cannot copy itself without activated building blocks, catalysts, and energy — a metabolism.
2. **Metabolism needs heredity.** A metabolism is a set of catalysts; to be maintained and improved across generations, the recipe for those catalysts has to be stored and transmitted — heredity.
3. **Both need a compartment.** Molecules that help each other must stay together, or they diffuse away and dilute to nothing; and a compartment is what lets one bundle of chemistry out-compete another.

Stated this way, each piece seems to presuppose the other two, and the field has largely split into camps that each start from one egg and struggle to reach the others: **replicator-first** ("RNA world"), **metabolism-first** (autocatalytic networks, hydrothermal vents), and **compartment-first** (lipid world, protocells). Each camp inherits the weaknesses of its starting point (Table 2).

The Cofactor Ratchet dissolves the knot by denying its premise. The three "eggs" are not separate objects that must be assembled in the right order. They are **three descriptions of one droplet**, and each description is trivially satisfied at the start because the droplet is simple. The hard problem is not *getting all three at once* — a droplet has all three the moment it exists — the hard problem is **growing the heredity channel from something that carries almost no information into something that carries a genome**. That is the one place a genuine transition has to happen, and it is the place this hypothesis puts its novel mechanism.

## The mechanism, step by step

### Step 1 — A lifeless soup with a fuel

The starting inventory is uncontroversial and prebiotically supported: amphiphiles (fatty acids, and abiotic "messy" polyesters), nucleotides and their precursors, sugars, amino acids, and a **source of chemical potential** — activated molecules, wet–dry cycling, or redox/pH gradients. Prebiotic routes to activated pyrimidine ribonucleotides (Powner, Gerland & Sutherland 2009; **DOI 10.1038/nature08013**) and to purine/pyrimidine nucleosides (Xu et al. 2020; **10.1038/s41586-020-2330-9**) mean that nucleotides — the raw material of both cofactors *and* genes — are present in the soup, not assumed into it.

### Step 2 — The soup phase-separates into an active droplet

Mixtures of oppositely-charged or amphiphilic organics spontaneously undergo **liquid–liquid phase separation** into dense droplets (coacervates). Crucially, these need not be passive: when a chemical fuel drives the production of the droplet's own material, the droplet becomes an **active droplet** that grows, maintains itself against Ostwald ripening, and divides when it gets too big — a purely physical growth-and-division cycle with no genes required (Zwicker, Seyboldt, Weber, Hyman & Jülicher 2017, *Nature Physics*, **10.1038/nphys3984**; Nakashima, van Haren, André, Robu & Spruijt 2021, *Nature Communications*, **10.1038/s41467-021-24111-x**). This single object already has a **compartment** (its interface), a **metabolism** (the fuelled reactions that make its material), and a proto-**division** cycle.

Coacervates are not inert bags: they concentrate RNA and dramatically enhance ribozyme and template-directed chemistry inside themselves (Drobot et al. 2018, **10.1038/s41467-018-06072-w**; Poudyal et al. 2019, **10.1038/s41467-019-08353-4**). Whatever chemistry the soup can do, the droplet does it faster and at higher local concentration.

### Step 3 — Nucleotide cofactors run the metabolism — and *are* the growth

Here is the first load-bearing claim. The catalysts that ran the earliest metabolism were not proteins (no translation yet) and need not have been full ribozymes. They were **small nucleotide-derived cofactors** — the chemical ancestors of ATP, NAD, FAD, coenzyme A, SAM, and the rest (Table 1). This is not a new speculation in isolation: the observation that most universal cofactors carry a gratuitous nucleotide (usually an adenosine) that plays *no chemical role* in their reaction is one of the oldest and best "molecular fossils" we have — a leftover handle from a time when the catalyst *was* a nucleotide (White 1976, *J. Mol. Evol.*, **10.1007/BF01732468**; modern synthesis: Goldman & Kaçar 2021, *J. Mol. Evol.*, **10.1007/s00239-020-09988-4**).

The novel move is to make these cofactors **the coupling between metabolism and compartment**: the cofactor-catalysed reactions produce more amphiphile and more scaffold, i.e. *cofactor metabolism = droplet growth*. A droplet that happens to contain a better set of cofactors grows and divides faster. Metabolism and reproduction are now the same event.

### Step 4 — Composition becomes heritable: the first, crude heredity

When an active droplet divides, its contents partition between the daughters. If the internal chemistry is **mutually catalytic** — species that help make each other, exactly the cofactor picture above — then the *composition* of a droplet is not random: it settles into self-reinforcing attractor states and those states are **transmitted to the offspring**. This is the GARD model of Segré, Ben-Eli & Lancet (2000, *PNAS*, **10.1073/pnas.97.8.4112**; review: Lancet, Zidovetzki & Markovitch 2018, *J. R. Soc. Interface*, **10.1098/rsif.2018.0159**), and it gives **heredity and selection with no genetic polymer at all** — "compositional inheritance."

We reproduced this (Figure 2a): a droplet lineage settles onto a stable **composome** (self-similarity across generations H = 0.99) that is sharply distinct from the surrounding soup, and that composition is transmitted parent-to-offspring across division far above chance (H = 0.99 for related droplets vs H = 0.12 for random assemblies of the same size).

### Step 5 — The compositional ceiling: why the droplet cannot become a cell *this way*

Compositional inheritance is real, but it has a fatal limitation that is well established in the literature and that we reproduced directly. The number of *distinct* heritable composome states is small and — the decisive point — **does not grow as you enlarge the molecular repertoire** (Figure 2b). Across repertoire sizes from N = 20 to N = 200, the count of distinct attractors stayed in the single digits, i.e. **~1–3 bits of heritable information**, regardless of N. A copyable sequence over the same N positions would carry **2N bits** (40–400 bits). This is the quantitative form of the Vasas, Szathmáry & Santos (2010, *PNAS*, **10.1073/pnas.0912628107**) result that self-sustaining compositional/autocatalytic systems lack the heritable variation needed for open-ended evolution. **A compositional protocell can be selected, but it cannot accumulate unbounded adaptation. It hits a wall.**

This wall is not a weakness of the hypothesis — it is the engine of it. It tells us *exactly when and why* a sequence-based heredity becomes valuable, and therefore selectable.

### Step 6 — The heredity handoff: cofactors ligate into the first templates (the novel core)

The cofactors that run the metabolism **are nucleotides**. Inside a crowded coacervate they are held at high local concentration on charged and ordered scaffolds. Under exactly these conditions — molecular crowding and physical (liquid-crystal-like) ordering of short oligonucleotides — **non-enzymatic, template-influenced ligation of oligonucleotides occurs abiotically** (Fraccia et al. 2015, *Nature Communications*, **10.1038/ncomms7424**; Jia & Fraccia 2020, **10.3390/cryst10110964**), and template-directed copying chemistry runs inside model protocells (Adamala & Szostak 2013, *Science*, **10.1126/science.1241888**).

So the droplet's own metabolic components, simply by being concentrated nucleotides, occasionally **stitch themselves into short templates**. At first this is a side-reaction with no benefit. But the moment a template happens to encode something that biases the droplet toward a *better* composome — even weakly — it provides heritable information **in a channel that is not subject to the compositional ceiling**. A template can be *lengthened*; a composome cannot. This is the handoff: **heredity is transferred from composition to sequence at the ceiling, and only there.**

We simulated the handoff in an evolving population of protocells (Figure 3). While heredity is compositional, the population's adaptation climbs and then **plateaus exactly at the compositional ceiling** and stays there indefinitely (the grey control line is flat for 1,200 generations — this is not slow progress, it is a wall). When a ligation event hands heredity to a copyable template, adaptation **breaks through the ceiling** and keeps climbing (red), and the heritable information carried by the lineage jumps from ~3 bits to ~27 bits. Across independent runs the breakthrough is robust and reaches ~3× above the compositional ceiling.

(b) Heritable adaptive information: compositional (grey) saturates at log2(C) ~ 3 bits; after the handoff the sequence channel (red) carries an unbounded and growing amount.](figures/fig3_heredity_handoff.png)

### Step 7 — Why the first template can be short enough to survive: the Eigen threshold

A sceptic asks: copying is error-prone, and Eigen (1971, *Naturwissenschaften*, **10.1007/BF00623322**) showed that a template longer than L_max ≈ ln(σ)/(1−q) cannot be maintained at per-site fidelity q. Doesn't the first genome need impossibly good copying?

No — and this is why the handoff is placed where it is. The **first** template does not need to be a ribozyme. It needs to carry only slightly more than the compositional ceiling — a **handful of sites** (Figure 2b/Panel-A analysis: even a 2–4-site template exceeds the entire compositional attractor repertoire). Figure 4 shows the Eigen bound: a ~4-site template survives at per-site error as high as ~50%, which is achievable by sloppy non-enzymatic ligation. Once such a template exists and confers any advantage, selection favours droplets whose cofactors copy it slightly better; better copying raises q, which raises L_max, which allows a longer template, which can encode a better copier — a **positive feedback that lengthens the genome and improves the catalysts together.** The ratchet clicks forward.

![Figure 4. The Eigen error threshold. The maximum maintainable template length grows as copying fidelity improves. A first template of only ~4 sites (the handoff length) sits far below the threshold even at ~50% error per site, so it can persist with purely non-enzymatic copying. Improving fidelity then lengthens the maintainable genome, opening the climb toward ribozyme-scale (~200 nt) templates that require q > 0.99.](figures/fig4_eigen_threshold.png)

### Step 8 — The membrane closes last

The active coacervate already has a functional interface, so a lipid bilayer is not needed to *start* — which sidesteps the hardest problem of the pure lipid-world and vesicle-first models. Instead, amphiphiles produced by the metabolism accumulate at the droplet interface and, past a threshold, the coacervate acquires a membrane (the coacervate-to-vesicle / membranised-coacervate transition is now demonstrated experimentally). The membrane is an **upgrade** selected because it stabilises the droplet against dilution and lets it hold a controllable boundary — plausibly the very selection pressure supplied by wet–dry and rainfall cycling on early Earth (Damer & Deamer 2019, **10.1089/ast.2019.2045**; Agrawal et al. 2024, *Science Advances*, **10.1126/sciadv.adn9657**; Ianeselli et al. 2021, **10.1101/2021.07.08.451414**). Compartment was there from the first line; the *good* compartment is the last thing to fall into place, not the first.

## What is genuinely new here

The individual ingredients are all real and published. The novelty is in the **architecture** that connects them:

1. **The cofactor as the pivot, not RNA and not a metabolic cycle.** Existing models treat cofactors as late add-ons. Here they are the central actor: the same molecules are simultaneously the metabolism's catalysts, the compartment's growth chemistry, and — because they are nucleotides — the raw material of the first genes. The "useless" nucleotide handle on NAD/FAD/CoA/SAM is not a curiosity; it is the fingerprint of the molecule that bridged metabolism and heredity.

2. **The "heredity handoff" as a specific, timed, testable transition.** The hypothesis does not hand-wave the origin of genetic information. It predicts that sequence heredity is recruited *precisely* when compositional heredity saturates, because that is the only moment sequence information has a selective advantage over the incumbent system. This converts "and then RNA appeared" — the weakest sentence in origin-of-life narratives — into a *derived* consequence of a ceiling we can calculate.

3. **A continuous ratchet with no improbable leap.** Every step is individually favoured and hard to reverse (Figure 1). There is no moment requiring a fully-formed replicator, a complete metabolic cycle, or a self-copying ribozyme to appear at once. The improbable "self-replicating cell" is reached as the *accumulated* product of many small, chemically-ordinary clicks.

4. **It unifies the warring camps instead of choosing one.** Replicator-first, metabolism-first, and compartment-first are recovered as three views of the same droplet at different stages of the ratchet (Table 2), which is why each camp captured part of the truth.

## Testable predictions

A hypothesis earns its keep by risking falsification. The Cofactor Ratchet makes concrete, laboratory-checkable predictions:

1. **Cofactor-driven growth.** An active coacervate fed a nucleotide-cofactor-catalysed reaction that produces its own amphiphile/scaffold will show sustained growth-and-division cycles whose *rate* tracks the intra-droplet cofactor concentration. Removing the cofactor stops growth without changing bulk thermodynamics.
2. **Compositional selection with a measurable ceiling.** Serial propagation (grow → divide → dilute) of such droplets will show heritable compositional drift toward fitter composomes that **saturates** — the fitness plateau should scale with repertoire diversity only weakly, matching Figure 2b.
3. **The handoff is observable.** In droplets held at high nucleotide concentration and physical ordering, short oligonucleotide ligation products will appear, and lineages that acquire a template that biases composition will break through the compositional fitness plateau (the signature of Figure 3). This is the single most important experiment: **catch a protocell lineage in the act of trading compositional heredity for sequence heredity.**
4. **Fidelity–length co-evolution.** Once templates appear, selection for droplet fitness will drive measurable increases in copying fidelity and, in lockstep, template length — the Eigen ratchet of Figure 4.
5. **Molecular-fossil corollary.** The earliest functional templates and cofactors should be built from the *same* nucleotide chemistry; reconstructed ancestral cofactor-utilising motifs should predate protein folds, consistent with cofactors as remnants (Goldman & Kaçar 2021).

## What would falsify it

- If active coacervates **cannot** couple an internal cofactor-catalysed reaction to their own growth (i.e. metabolism and compartment cannot be made one object), the central pivot fails.
- If compositional inheritance shows **no** ceiling — if heritable information grows without bound as repertoire grows — then sequence heredity is not needed and the "handoff" is unmotivated.
- If short (2–5 nt) templates provide **no** selectable advantage to a droplet lineage at any achievable fidelity, the ratchet has no first tooth.
- If nucleotide cofactors turn out to be strictly **downstream** of coded protein metabolism in every reconstruction (no fossil signal), the "cofactor as bridge" claim is undercut.

## Honest limitations

This is a **hypothesis**, argued from real chemistry and quantified with deliberately simple models — not a demonstrated pathway. The origin of life remains unsolved. Specifically: (i) our GARD and ratchet simulations are abstract, chosen to isolate the *logic* of the compositional ceiling and the handoff, not to reproduce a specific chemistry; the ceiling result reproduces a known qualitative finding, and the handoff is a proof-of-concept that a sequence channel breaks a compositional plateau, not a molecular simulation. (ii) The specific first cofactor-catalysed growth reaction is not identified; we argue it is *plausible and prebiotically reachable*, not *known*. (iii) The handoff step — spontaneous ligation of cofactor-nucleotides into a template that then biases composition — is the least experimentally constrained link and is exactly where we direct the key experiment. The value of the hypothesis is that it makes the hard step **small, timed, and testable** rather than miraculous.
